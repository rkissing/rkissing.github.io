[ Manual-Interface, Dexterity-Affording Solution ]

On Saturday, April 28, 2018, a fatal DUI crash in south Los Angeles. Around 7:30 PM, a drunk driver collided with a silver minivan, and three people were killed in the accident—an adult, child, and an infant. The only survivors of the crash are Elizabeth and Julian Flores, and each hospitalized in critical condition. Due to the blazing heat, Julian suffered third degree burns and lost nine of his fingers. Through reference of close friends, their case has been brought to the attention of the Quality of Life Plus Student Association at Cal Poly, and a team of eight students endeavors to restore the freedom of childhood and creativity by providing Julian a set of prosthetic hands.

Quarter 1 | DESIGN

Turbulent Beginnings

Project Midas was incredibly rapid at the outset. The scope of the project was substantial and heavy: an immense human factor that attracted applicants to give their best efforts to the project. The process began in the fall. I applied as a Team Lead applicant to the Quality of Life Plus Student Association board; under reccomendation from my previous work on the ALPS project, I was selected for this project, handed a stack of nearly seventy resumes, conducting interviews for almost three hours to ensure the proper team was selected for the project.

The team I selected was evenly distributed in abilities, representing a variety of Cal Poly students well-versed in Mechatronics, Biomedical Engineering, and Mechanical Design. The focus of the Mechatronics students was on developing the electronic components, code in either Arduino, Python, or C++ environments, and testing electrical components for satisfactory performance. The Biomedical team focused on developing molds, gathering anthropometric data, and developing biotolerant systems that our challenger's hands could interface with. The Mechanical Design team comprised of developing the solid models and testing the mechanical workings of both hands.

Initial Considerations

I conducted extensive research before the first meeting to ensure my team had the maximum possible momentum going forward with the project; from previous experience, the start largely determined the overall pace of the project. I knew that various configurations were necessary for Julian's hands. The following figures demonstrate some of the necessary positions our team considered.

The picture below shows Julian's hands at the start of the project.


The first crucial distinction that our team made was that Julian's hands were at two different levels of disability: the right hand still had the majority of the proximal still remaining, while the left hand had nearly no motion, and no fingers. This realization prompted us to divide the project into two distinct projects. Although the initial assumption of the project was that the design for the left could be mirrored to the right, this was proven to be largely false. Thus became the divison into a left-hand mechatronics team and a right-hand (purely mechanical) design team.
